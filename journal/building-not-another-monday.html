Building Not Another Monday as our first commissioned app

Not Another Monday was our first paid commission, and it taught us a lot about building a feature-rich wellness app that still feels calm, personal, and trustworthy.

Commissioned Wellness App Not Another Monday Consistent action. Better you.

Why this project mattered

Every project teaches you something, but some of them mark a real shift. Not Another Monday was one of those projects for us. It was our first paid commissioned build, which meant we were not only shaping a product, we were helping another entity bring its vision, tone, and standards into a real app people could rely on.

That changes the weight of the work in a good way. It raises the bar for communication, clarity, and follow-through. It also reminds you that the product is not just a technical build. It represents someone's brand, audience, and trust.

The real challenge was simplicity

Not Another Monday was never meant to feel like just another tracker. It needed to cover habits, weight, water, steps, fasting, stretching, journaling, progress photos, goals, achievements, and account syncing without becoming noisy or stressful.

That was the hardest part of the product: keeping the surface calm while a lot was happening underneath. Simplicity sounds light, but it takes real discipline to make something feel effortless when the feature set is broad.

What sat underneath the calm UI

  • Daily habit tracking with streak logic and progress summaries.
  • Weight, body, hydration, movement, fasting, and wellness support.
  • One-line journaling, reflection, and shareable progress moments.
  • Onboarding, profile setup, syncing, and account-level trust.

Cross-platform meant real discipline

Flutter gave us a strong foundation, but cross-platform work still asks a lot from a team. We had to work through layout and overflow issues on smaller phones, authentication flow, splash behavior, app icons, release preparation, and the platform-specific details that show up once a product leaves the ideal screen size.

A clean interface is only convincing if it keeps working everywhere. A lot of the build effort went into making sure the app felt stable, responsive, and deliberate across both iOS and Android.

Progress had to feel human

A habit and health app can become mechanical very quickly. That was never the goal here. The emotional side of the product mattered as much as the functional side. The home progress ring, streaks, journaling, progress photos, and Life Moving Forward moments all needed to feel supportive rather than clinical.

That meant revisiting wording, hierarchy, spacing, and interaction patterns many times. We were not just asking whether a feature worked. We were asking how it felt when someone used it on a good day, a flat day, or a day where they were trying to start again.

Working with another person changes the job

One of the most valuable parts of the project was learning through real collaboration. Building your own ideas is one thing. Building with another person means listening carefully, translating vision into structure, and making sure the final product still feels like it belongs to the brand it was created for.

It taught us a lot about alignment. You have to protect usability, respect the emotional direction of the product, and stay open to feedback without losing the clarity of the build. That balance is part of what makes commissioned work worth doing well.

What we learned

The clearest lesson from Not Another Monday was that simplicity is hard. It takes more restraint, more iteration, and more technical thought to make a product feel calm than it does to keep adding more visible complexity.

  • Simplicity is not small work. It is focused work.
  • Emotional design matters just as much as technical functionality in wellness products.
  • Trust depends on persistence, sync accuracy, and a reliable daily flow.
  • Commissioned work sharpens communication, accountability, and product judgment.

We are proud of what the app became, and equally proud of what the project taught us. It pushed us to build something more human, more polished, and more disciplined than a standard tracker. That is exactly the kind of work we want to keep doing.
